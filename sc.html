Oceans cover 71% of our planet and are home to countless species. They regulate our climate, produce oxygen, and provide food for billions. Protecting marine life is essential for maintaining the balance of our entire ecosystem.

  • Plastic Pollution: 8 million tons of plastic enter oceans yearly
  • Overfishing: Depletes fish populations faster than they can reproduce
  • Climate Change: Warmer waters and acidification harm coral reefs
  • Habitat Destruction: Coastal development destroys critical ecosystems
  1. Reduce single-use plastics in your daily life
  2. Choose sustainable seafood options
  3. Participate in beach cleanups
  4. Support marine conservation organizations
  5. Educate others about ocean issues

Frequently Asked Questions

What marine animals are most at risk?

Sea turtles, coral reefs, whales, sharks, and many fish species are particularly vulnerable due to human activities like fishing, pollution, and climate change.

How does plastic harm ocean animals?

Animals often mistake plastic for food, which can cause starvation when their digestive systems become blocked. Many also become entangled in plastic debris.

Are coral reefs really dying?

Yes, about 50% of the world's coral reefs have died in the last 30 years due to warming waters, acidification, and pollution.

What percentage of oxygen comes from the ocean?

Marine plants, especially phytoplankton, produce 50-80% of the Earth's oxygen through photosynthesis.

Sound shapes ocean life

01

"What happens when the ocean is no longer silent — but screaming ways we can't hear?"

Sound Pollution

  1. In the ocean, it is an invisible threat that disturbs marine life in profound ways. Unlike land, were noise is often contained, under water sound travels much farther and faster, disrupting communication, navigation and the natural balance of ecosystems.

  2. Ocean sound pollution occurs when human-made noises — like ships engines, sonar, seismic surveys, and industrial activities — interfere with the natural acoustic environment. This noise pollution poses a significant risk to marine species, especially those reliant on echolocation and communitcation.

Disrupted Communication
Effect

Marine animals struggle to communicate due to noise pollution

Noise from ships and sonar disrupts echolocation and communication.

Stress and Displacement
Effect

Marine life experiences stress and is forced to move away from habitats

Noise pollution causes behavioral changes and habitat displacement.

Physical Harm
Effect

Noise pollution can cause physical injuries to marine animals

Loud noises can damage hearing and cause stress-related health issues.

Case Studies

01
Early Recognition of Underwater Noise Pollution
1960s - 1970s

Scientists began to document the increasing levels of human-made noise in the oceans, primarily from shipping and naval sonar, and its potential effects on marine life.

02
Impact on Marine Mammals
1980s - 1990s

Research revealed that noise pollution disrupts communication, navigation, and feeding behaviors of whales, dolphins, and other marine mammals, leading to strandings and population declines.

03
Expansion of Noise Sources
2000s

Industrial activities such as seismic surveys for oil and gas exploration, increased shipping traffic, and construction introduced new and louder noise sources, exacerbating impacts on marine ecosystems.

04
Recognition of Broader Ecological Effects
2010s

Studies showed that noise pollution affects not only marine mammals but also fish, invertebrates, and entire ecosystems, altering behaviors, reproduction, and predator-prey relationships.

05
Mitigation and Regulation Efforts
2020s and Beyond

Implementation of quieter ship technologies, marine protected areas, and international regulations aim to reduce underwater noise and protect marine life from further harm.

Reduce Ship Noise

Quieter ship technologies such as improved propeller designs and hull modifications can significantly reduce underwater noise. Slowing down vessel speeds also decreases noise pollution, benefiting marine life.

Establish Quiet Zones

Marine protected areas with enforced noise restrictions help safeguard critical habitats from disruptive human-made sounds, allowing ecosystems to recover and thrive.

Regulate Industrial Noise

Limiting seismic surveys and construction noise during sensitive breeding and migration periods reduces stress and harm to marine species.
